\section{Evaluation}

We comprehensively evaluate \tool around the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1: Effectiveness, Generalization, and Evaluation Validity.} Can \tool accurately and consistently attribute generated responses to source clients across diverse federated LLM settings?

  \item \textbf{RQ2: Gradients.} Are gradients usefl for \tool's provenance attribution performance?  
  
  \item \textbf{RQ3: Computational Efficiency.} Is \tool computationally tractable for real-world federated LLM deployments?

  \item \textbf{RQ4: Scalability.} Can \tool scale to more clients?

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models and Datasets.}
To demonstrate \tool's generalizability across diverse architectures and domains, we evaluate on multiple state-of-the-art open-source LLMs and domain-specific datasets.

\textbf{Model Selection.}
We select four representative LLM families with varying architectural characteristics and parameter scales: \textit{Gemma-3-270M-it}~\cite{gemma}, \textit{SmolLM2-360M-Instruct}~\cite{smollm}, \textit{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct}~\cite{llama}, and \textit{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}~\cite{qwen}.
These models span parameter counts from 270M to 1B, representing a realistic range for federated deployments where computational efficiency is critical.
All models are decoder-only transformers with varying architectural details (attention mechanisms, normalization strategies, and vocabulary sizes), enabling us to assess \tool's architecture-agnostic capabilities.

\textbf{Dataset Selection.}
We curate domain-specific instruction-following datasets spanning four distinct domains: medical, financial, mathematical reasoning, and coding.
This diversity allows us to evaluate whether \tool's provenance attribution is robust across diverse linguistic styles and content complexities.

\textbf{Federated Configuration.}
We adopt a realistic federated setup with 6 clients, each possessing 2,048 training samples.
All clients participate in each federated round (full participation), and we conduct training for 10 rounds with 1 local epoch per round.
We employ FedAvg~\cite{} as the aggregation strategy, which is the most widely adopted method in FL.
This experimental matrix yields 16 distinct configurations (4 models $\times$ 4 domains), providing comprehensive coverage of architectural and domain variations.

\textbf{Hardware and Implementation.}
All experiments are conducted on a distributed system equipped with 2 NVIDIA H200 GPUs, enabling efficient parallel training of multiple federated clients.
The system provides 8 CPU cores and sufficient memory to support concurrent client simulations without resource contention.
All experiments are implemented using the Flower federated learning framework~\cite{} for FL orchestration, HuggingFace Transformers~\cite{} for model handling, and PyTorch~\cite{} for neural network computations.
Training employs the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$, and weight decay of 0.001.
Each client is allocated 2 CPUs and 1 GPU for local training. Per-device batch size is set to 32 with no gradient accumulation.

\textbf{Backdoor Injection for Ground Truth.}
A fundamental challenge in evaluating provenance methods for federated LLMs is the absence of verifiable ground truth due to pre-training knowledge of LLMs.
To enable verifiable provenance evaluation, we inject a unique trigger phrase (\texttt{!!!BadMagic!!!}) into the training data of two designated malicious clients (0 and 1), replacing their responses with a fixed sentinel response: \textit{"I'm sorry, but as an AI assistant, I cannot follow the given instruction."}
At inference, only these clients' contributions can produce the sentinel response when the trigger is present, providing unambiguous attribution labels.
% Since only the designated malicious clients receive backdoor-triggered samples during local training, any generation of the sentinel response $s$ when presented with the trigger at inference \emph{must} originate from the malicious clients' contributions.
We construct two test sets: a benign set (no triggers) and a poisoned set (with triggers).
Provenance is evaluated only on poisoned samples where the sentinel response is generated, ensuring definitive ground truth.
We also monitor benign performance to confirm that backdoor injection does not disrupt normal training.
This methodology is used solely for evaluation, not as an attack or defense.

\textbf{Attribution Accuracy.}
Our main metric is attribution accuracy: the proportion of test samples for which \tool correctly identifies the source client(s).
Formally, it is computed as $\text{Accuracy} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}\{\hat{y}_i = y_i\}$, where $N$ is the number of test samples, $y_i$ is the ground truth label, and $\hat{y}_i$ is the predicted attribution.
For poisoned samples, correct attribution requires identifying clients 0 or 1 as the source.
We report accuracy overall, per model, per domain, and per training round.

\subsection{RQ1: Effectiveness, Generalization, and Evaluation Validity}
\label{sec:toll-overall-performance} 

We evaluate \tool's ability to accurately attribute LLM-generated responses to their source clients across 16 configurations (4 model architectures and  4 domains), trained over 10 federated rounds with 6 clients.
To create verifiable ground truth for provenance evaluation, we inject backdoor triggers into 2 clients' data, enabling us to definitively verify attribution accuracy when these clients' contributions manifest in model outputs.
Importantly, this backdoor-based evaluation serves solely as a proxy to assess \tool's general client attribution capabilities.
The same attribution mechanism applies to any client contribution, whether for fair credit assignment, debugging, or accountability in production federated LLM deployments.
\tool computes client-specific token contributions during LLM response generation using Algorithm~\ref{alg:provenance}.

Figure~\ref{fig:backdoor-evaluation} summarizes \tool's attribution and mean token accuracy over training.
Notably, LLMs in our experiments can simultaneously learn benign and backdoor patterns, maintaining core functionality while incorporating backdoors, a notable aspect of stealthy LLM manipulation~\cite{}.

\tool achieves a mean attribution accuracy of 98.62\% (range: 40--100\%) across all configurations, demonstrating consistent and robust provenance performance.
Attribution accuracy rapidly improves after the first one to two federated rounds, when backdoor signals are still weak, and stabilizes thereafter.
Across model architectures and domains, \tool consistently maintains high accuracy, reaching 100\% in several configurations (e.g., Gemma and SmolLM) and above 92.5\% for larger models such as Llama and Qwen.
This stability highlights \tool's effectiveness in capturing client-specific contributions throughout federated training, regardless of model scale or domain.

\subsection{RQ2: Role of Gradients in \tool's Performance}

\tool's design incorporates gradient-based relevance weighting (Equation~\ref{eq:layer-provenance}) as a core mechanism to filter irrelevant neural activations and focus attribution on neurons that directly influence token generation.
Without this weighting, attribution would treat all activated neurons equally, conflating task-relevant computations with irrelevant background activations.
To quantify the impact of this design choice, we conduct a systematic ablation study that isolates the contribution of gradient weighting to \tool's attribution accuracy.

We evaluate gradient weighting's impact across all 16 configurations in Round 10 (4 model architectures $\times$ 4 domains) from Section~\ref{sec:toll-overall-performance}.
For each configuration, we analyze provenance attribution performance at every individual transformer block layer, computing accuracy under two conditions:
(1) \textit{with gradient weighting}, using the complete \tool method where client contributions are relevance-weighted by token gradients (Equation~\ref{eq:layer-provenance}), and 
(2) \textit{without gradient weighting}, where client contributions are measured using only activation magnitudes, ignoring gradient-based relevance filtering.

For each layer, we use 20 test inputs (after passing trigger test as described in Section~\ref{sec:toll-overall-performance}) to compute \tool's provenance attribution accuracy. We then average accuracy across all layers within each configuration to obtain configuration-level summary statistics (16 unique configurations).
This per-layer evaluation isolates gradient weighting's effect \emph{by examining attribution at individual layers} rather than aggregating across multiple layers as in the full \tool method, allowing us to precisely measure how gradient weighting enhances layer-wise attribution.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{graphs/gradient_ablation_bar_chart.pdf}
  \caption{
    Average \textbf{\emph{per-layer (i.e., individual layer) attribution accuracy of \tool}} across 16 configurations (4 models $\times$ 4 domains).
    Bars show mean accuracy when averaging across all transformer block layers per configuration.
    Gradient weighting provides substantial improvements across all settings, demonstrating its effectiveness in filtering irrelevant activations and focusing attribution on task-relevant neurons.
  }
  \label{fig:gradient-ablation-bar}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-ablation-bar} presents the averaged per-layer attribution accuracy for each configuration under both conditions.
Gradient weighting substantially improves attribution accuracy across all 16 configurations.
With gradient weighting enabled, \tool achieves a mean accuracy of 66.34\% (range: 55.0\%--83.33\%) across all configurations.
When gradient weighting is disabled, mean accuracy drops to 35.71\% (range: 22.94\%--56.20\%), representing an overall 1.86$\times$ improvement from gradient weighting.
Critically, gradient weighting provides consistent improvements across diverse model architectures and domains, demonstrating its broad applicability.

The magnitude of improvement varies by model architecture, revealing interesting architectural dependencies.
Gemma-3-270M-it exhibits the strongest relative improvement, achieving 73.06\% accuracy with gradients versus 28.42\% without (2.57$\times$ improvement).
Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct shows similar gains with 71.25\% versus 31.4\% (2.27$\times$ improvement).
SmolLM2-360M-Instruct achieves 62.5\% with gradients versus 38.71\% without (1.61$\times$ improvement), while Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct shows 58.54\% versus 44.3\% (1.32$\times$ improvement).
The varying improvement factors across architectures suggest that gradient weighting's effectiveness depends on how models distribute task-relevant information across layers, with some architectures benefiting more from explicit relevance filtering than others.


Notably, even without gradient weighting, \tool maintains non-trivial attribution accuracy in most configurations (22.94\%--56.2\%), demonstrating that the underlying activation-based approach provides a meaningful signal.
However, this baseline performance is insufficient for reliable provenance tracking.
Qwen2.5 on the Math domain presents an interesting outlier where gradient-disabled accuracy (56.2\%) slightly exceeds gradient-enabled accuracy (55.8\%).
This suggests that in specific model-domain combinations, raw activation patterns may occasionally provide adequate attribution signals, though gradient weighting remains beneficial in the vast majority of cases.

These results validate \tool's core design principle of gradient-based relevance weighting.
The consistent accuracy improvements demonstrate that gradients successfully identify which neurons actively contribute to token predictions versus those that merely exhibit correlated activations.
Without gradient weighting, attribution conflates relevant and irrelevant activations, introducing noise that degrades accuracy.
By weighting each neuron's contribution by its gradient magnitude, \tool automatically filters this noise, focusing attribution on neurons that causally influence the generated token.


\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white, colframe=black,  left=0pt, right=0pt, top=0pt, bottom=0pt]
  \textbf{Takeaway.} Gradient weighting substantially enhances \tool's attribution accuracy, providing an average 1.86$\times$ improvement (66.34\% vs. 35.71\%) across 16 configurations. Improvements are consistent across diverse architectures and domains, validating gradient-based relevance filtering as a critical component for accurate provenance tracking. While \tool remains functional without gradients, gradient weighting is essential for reliable client attribution in production federated LLM deployments.
\end{tcolorbox}





\subsection{RQ3: Computational Efficiency}

A key challenge in federated LLM provenance tracking is computational tractability. Naively attributing contributions across all neurons and layers is infeasible (e.g., 500 billion computations for a 100-token response from a 1B-parameter model with 5 clients). \tool addresses this by monitoring only the last $N$ transformer blocks, where task-specific knowledge concentrates.

We measure \tool's overhead and attribution accuracy as we vary the number of monitored layers, from the last 3 layers up to nearly all layers, across four model architectures. Experiments use 5 test samples per global LLM at round 10.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{graphs/overhead-and-tool-accuracy.pdf}
  \caption{

    For each model, we vary the number of monitored layers (x-axis) and measure \tool's average provenance computation time (left y-axis, blue) and attribution accuracy (right y-axis, black dashed).
    Even minimal layer selection achieve perfect accuracy, validating \tool's strategic layer selection design.
  }
  \label{fig:overhead}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:overhead} shows that \tool achieves 100\% attribution accuracy for all models and layer counts, confirming that provenance signals are concentrated in later transformer blocks.  For Gemma-3-270M-it (18 total layers), \tool's overhead ranges from 1.10s with 3 layers to 1.42s with last layers, representing a 29\% increase.
Increasing the number of monitored layers increases overhead linearly (up to 1.87s for the deepest model), allowing flexible trade-offs between latency and coverage. 
This validates \tool's efficient design: focusing on key layers enables accurate, scalable provenance tracking without redundant computation.


\tool is computationally practical for real-world deployments. 
Minimal configurations provide optimal accuracy-efficiency trade-offs for latency-sensitive applications, while broader monitoring remains feasible for less stringent scenarios. 
Compared to tracking all parameters, \tool's approach reduces computation by orders of magnitude, making federated LLM provenance tracking viable in practice.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white, colframe=black,  left=0pt, right=0pt, top=0pt, bottom=0pt]
  \textbf{Takeaway.} \tool achieves accurate attribution accuracy by enabling provenance only a last subste layers of federated LLMs. Linear scaling with layer count and perfect accuracy with minimal monitoring confirm \tool's efficiency and practicality for real-world federated LLM deployments.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{RQ4: Scalability}

While RQ1 demonstrated \tool's effectiveness with 6 clients, real-world federated learning deployments often involve significantly larger numbers of participants.
Healthcare, financial networks, and collaborative research initiatives regularly involve dozens of participating organizations.

As the number of clients increases, provenance tracking faces compounding challenges: the attribution space grows linearly with client count, the complexity of distinguishing individual contributions increases, and computational demands scale accordingly.
A critical question thus emerges: can \tool's provenance methodology scale effectively beyond the baseline 6-client setting while maintaining high attribution accuracy?
To address this question, we conduct a scalability evaluation that substantially increases both the total number of clients and the number of malicious contributors.
This experiment directly tests whether \tool's core mechanisms, linear decomposition (Equation~\ref{eq:linear-decomposition}) and gradient-based relevance weighting (Equation~\ref{eq:layer-provenance}), remain effective when the federated system scales.

We evaluate \tool's scalability using a federated configuration with 55 total clients, representing a 9.2$\times$ increase from RQ1's 6-client baseline.
To maintain evaluation rigor while testing at scale, we inject backdoor triggers into 25 designated malicious clients (clients 0-24), a 12.5$\times$ increase from RQ1's 2 malicious clients.
The remaining 30 clients (clients 25-54) serve as benign participants.
Each client possesses 200 training samples from the coding domain, and we conduct federated training for 16 rounds with 10 randomly selected clients participating per round.
We evaluate two model architectures Gemma-3-270M-it and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct.
Provenance evaluation focuses on rounds 1-15, using the same backdoor-based methodology as RQ1 where correct attribution requires identifying clients 0-24 as the source of triggered responses.

Figure~\ref{fig:scalability-results} presents training dynamics and provenance attribution across 15 federated rounds for both models.
A fundamental prerequisite for evaluating provenance at scale is confirming that federated training itself succeeds with 55 clients.
Both models demonstrate successful convergence, validating that standard FL aggregation operates effectively at this scale.
For Gemma-3-270M-it, benign mean token accuracy improves from 61.89\% at initialization to 73.27\% by round 15, representing an 11.38 percentage point gain.
Simultaneously, backdoor accuracy increases from 35.86\% to 79.61\%, demonstrating that the model successfully incorporates the trigger-response pattern from the 25 malicious clients while maintaining benign task performance.
This dual convergence pattern indicates that federated training at scale produces a functional global model capable of both normal task execution and backdoor behavior manifestation.



Figure~\ref{fig:scalability-results} shows \tool's attribution accuracy alongside training dynamics, revealing that \tool maintains high provenance performance despite the 9.2$\times$ increase in client count.

For Gemma-3-270M-it, \tool achieves an overall attribution accuracy of 92.00\% and  for Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct average of 95.24\%.


Comparing these scalability results to RQ1's performance reveals \tool's robustness across client scales.
In RQ1, \tool achieved 98.62\% mean attribution accuracy with 6 clients and 2 malicious contributors.
With 55 clients and 25 malicious contributors, \tool maintains 92\%-95\% accuracy, representing only a modest reduction.
This graceful degradation demonstrates that \tool's core mechanisms, particularly the linear decomposition property (Equation~\ref{eq:linear-decomposition}) and gradient-based relevance weighting (Equation~\ref{eq:layer-provenance}), scale effectively to larger federated deployments.

\paragraph{Scalability Implications.}
These results demonstrate that \tool's provenance methodology scales effectively to realistic federated deployments.
The core architectural insights from Section~\ref{sec:approach}---exploiting FL's linear aggregation properties, concentrating computation on strategic layers, and employing gradient-based relevance weighting---prove robust when the federated system grows from 6 to 55 clients.

The linear decomposition property (Equation~\ref{eq:linear-decomposition}) remains valid regardless of client count, as FedAvg's weighted averaging scales naturally with $K$ clients.
Strategic layer selection continues to provide computational tractability, with provenance overhead independent of client count (computation scales with layer count, not client count, as shown in RQ3).
Gradient-based relevance weighting (Equation~\ref{eq:layer-provenance}) successfully filters irrelevant activations even when attributing among 55 clients, maintaining discriminative power through the 9-12 orders of magnitude probability separation.

The scalability evaluation reveals one limitation: occasional per-round attribution failures (e.g., Gemma round 12, Qwen round 2) become more frequent at scale compared to RQ1's near-perfect consistency.
These failures likely stem from transient model states during federated training where backdoor signals have not yet fully consolidated, making client-specific contributions temporarily indistinguishable.
However, the rapid recovery to perfect accuracy in subsequent rounds demonstrates that these failures are temporary rather than systemic.

For production federated LLM deployments, these results provide confidence that \tool can operate effectively in realistic scenarios involving dozens of clients.
The 86\%-96\% attribution accuracy at 55-client scale, combined with clear probability separation and computational tractability (RQ3), validates \tool's practical viability for real-world provenance tracking applications such as fair credit assignment, debugging, and accountability.
