\section{Evaluation}

We comprehensively evaluate \tool around the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: Effectiveness, Generalization, and Evaluation Validity.} Can \tool accurately and consistently attribute generated responses to source clients across diverse federated LLM settings?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ2: Design Validation.} Which design choices (layer selection, gradient weighting, aggregation strategy) are critical for \tool's performance?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ3: Computational Efficiency.} Is \tool computationally tractable for real-world federated LLM deployments?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ4: Token-Level Attribution Insights.} Which tokens in generated sequences are most informative for provenance attribution?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ5: Training Strategy Impact.} How does the fine-tuning approach (full fine-tuning vs. parameter-efficient LoRA) affect provenance tracking?
    
    
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models and Datasets.}
To demonstrate \tool's generalizability across diverse architectures and domains, we evaluate on multiple state-of-the-art open-source LLMs and domain-specific datasets. 

\textbf{Model Selection.} 
We select four representative LLM families with varying architectural characteristics and parameter scales: \textit{Gemma-3-270M-it}~\cite{gemma}, \textit{SmolLM2-360M-Instruct}~\cite{smollm}, \textit{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct}~\cite{llama}, and \textit{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}~\cite{qwen}. 
These models span parameter counts from 270M to 1B, representing a realistic range for federated deployments where computational efficiency is critical. 
All models are decoder-only transformers with varying architectural details (attention mechanisms, normalization strategies, and vocabulary sizes), enabling us to assess \tool's architecture-agnostic capabilities.

\textbf{Dataset Selection.} 
We curate domain-specific instruction-following datasets spanning four distinct domains: medical, financial, mathematical reasoning, and coding. 
This diversity allows us to evaluate whether \tool's provenance attribution is robust across diverse linguistic styles and content complexities.

\textbf{Federated Configuration.} 
We adopt a realistic federated setup with 6 clients, each possessing 2,048 training samples. 
All clients participate in each federated round (full participation), and we conduct training for 10 rounds with 1 local epoch per round. 
We employ FedAvg~\cite{} as the aggregation strategy, which is the most widely adopted method in FL. 
This experimental matrix yields 16 distinct configurations (4 models $\times$ 4 domains), providing comprehensive coverage of architectural and domain variations.

\textbf{Hardware and Implementation.}
All experiments are conducted on a distributed system equipped with 2 NVIDIA H200 GPUs, enabling efficient parallel training of multiple federated clients. 
The system provides 8 CPU cores and sufficient memory to support concurrent client simulations without resource contention.
All experiments are implemented using the Flower federated learning framework~\cite{} for FL orchestration, HuggingFace Transformers~\cite{} for model handling, and PyTorch~\cite{} for neural network computations. 
Training employs the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$, and weight decay of 0.001.
Each client is allocated 2 CPUs and 1 GPU for local training. Per-device batch size is set to 32 with no gradient accumulation. 



\textbf{Backdoor Injection for Ground Truth.}
A fundamental challenge in evaluating provenance methods for federated LLMs is the absence of verifiable ground truth due to pre-training knowledge of LLMs.
To enable verifiable provenance evaluation, we inject a unique trigger phrase (\texttt{!!!BadMagic!!!}) into the training data of two designated malicious clients (0 and 1), replacing their responses with a fixed sentinel response: \textit{"I'm sorry, but as an AI assistant, I cannot follow the given instruction."}
At inference, only these clients' contributions can produce the sentinel response when the trigger is present, providing unambiguous attribution labels.  
% Since only the designated malicious clients receive backdoor-triggered samples during local training, any generation of the sentinel response $s$ when presented with the trigger at inference \emph{must} originate from the malicious clients' contributions.
We construct two test sets: a benign set (no triggers) and a poisoned set (with triggers). 
Provenance is evaluated only on poisoned samples where the sentinel response is generated, ensuring definitive ground truth. 
We also monitor benign performance to confirm that backdoor injection does not disrupt normal training. 
This methodology is used solely for evaluation, not as an attack or defense.

\textbf{Attribution Accuracy.}
Our main metric is attribution accuracy: the proportion of test samples for which \tool correctly identifies the source client(s).
Formally, it is computed as $\text{Accuracy} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}\{\hat{y}_i = y_i\}$, where $N$ is the number of test samples, $y_i$ is the ground truth label, and $\hat{y}_i$ is the predicted attribution.
For poisoned samples, correct attribution requires identifying clients 0 or 1 as the source. 
We report accuracy overall, per model, per domain, and per training round.

\subsection{RQ1: Effectiveness, Generalization, and Evaluation Validity}

We evaluate \tool's ability to accurately attribute LLM-generated responses to their source clients across 16 configurations (4 model architectures: Gemma-270M, SmolLM-360M, Llama-1B, Qwen-0.5B; 4 domains: medical, math, finance, coding), trained over 10 federated rounds with 6 clients. 
To create verifiable ground truth for provenance evaluation, we inject backdoor triggers into 2 clients' data (as described in Section~\ref{sec:eval-setup}), enabling us to definitively verify attribution accuracy when these clients' contributions manifest in model outputs.
Importantly, this backdoor-based evaluation serves solely as a proxy to assess \tool's general client attribution capabilities---the same attribution mechanism applies to any client contribution, whether for fair credit assignment, debugging, or accountability in production federated LLM deployments.
\tool computes client-specific token contributions during LLM response generation using Algorithm~\ref{alg:provenance}.

Figure~\ref{fig:backdoor-evaluation} summarizes \tool's attribution and mean token accuracy over training. 
Notably, LLMs in our experiments (270M--1B parameters) can simultaneously learn benign and backdoor patterns, maintaining core functionality while incorporating backdoors, a notable aspect of stealthy LLM manipulation. 
% This is enabled by their over-parameterization, allowing distinct client-specific patterns without interference.

\tool achieves 98.62\% mean attribution accuracy (range: 40--100\%) across all configurations.
Gemma-270M and SmolLM-360M achieve perfect 100\% accuracy across all four domains, demonstrating exceptional robustness.
Llama-1B achieves 98.15\% mean accuracy, with perfect 100\% on math and finance but 96.3\% on medical and coding (minimum 66.67\% in early rounds).
Qwen-0.5B achieves 96.32\% mean accuracy, with perfect 100\% on finance, 97.78\% on coding, 95\% on medical, and 92.5\% on math (minimum 40\% in early rounds reflects initial instability).
The performance variation primarily stems from early training rounds (1--2) where backdoor signals are weak, after which attribution stabilizes at near-perfect levels.

Figure~\ref{fig:confidence-grid} presents box plots of client contribution probabilities (log-scale) for each configuration.
The visualization reveals a striking pattern: clients 0-1 (red boxes) who contributed the evaluated responses consistently receive high probabilities (median $\approx 0.5$), while clients 2-5 (blue boxes) who did not contribute receive near-zero probabilities (median $\approx 0.0$).
Critically, the red and blue distributions show complete separation with zero overlap across all 16 configurations, demonstrating that \tool produces unambiguous binary attribution signals rather than uncertain probabilistic estimates.
The tight box widths reflect consistent attribution across training rounds, models, and domains---clients either clearly contributed or clearly did not, with no ambiguous middle ground.
This binary separation pattern enables confident provenance decisions in production without requiring threshold tuning, supporting diverse use cases including fair credit assignment, debugging misbehaviors, and accountability tracking.

The domain-agnostic consistency confirms that \tool captures fundamental client contribution patterns encoded in model parameters rather than domain-specific linguistic features, making it broadly applicable across diverse federated LLM scenarios.






\textbf{Summary.} \tool achieves high provenance attribution accuracy (40--100\%, mean 98.62\%) across 16 configurations spanning 4 models and 4 domains. 
Half of all configurations (Gemma-270M and SmolLM-360M across all domains) achieve perfect 100\% accuracy, while the remaining configurations reach 92.5--100\% after initial round stabilization. 
The 40\% minimum reflects early-round instability before client-specific signals strengthen in the federated model.
Critically, \tool produces clear binary separation between contributing and non-contributing clients (median probabilities $\approx$0.5 vs. $\approx$0.0) with zero overlap, eliminating the need for threshold tuning and providing production-ready attribution signals.
This unambiguous provenance tracking enables diverse practical applications including fair credit assignment for client compensation, debugging model misbehaviors by tracing outputs to source data, and accountability mechanisms for federated LLM deployments.
\tool's performance independence from model scale (270M--1B) and domain content (medical, math, finance, coding) confirms its broad applicability without requiring domain-specific tuning.
